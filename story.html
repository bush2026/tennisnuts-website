Our Story

How a game became a community.

Communities rarely begin with ambition. They begin with people who want to keep showing up. This is the story of how Tennisnuts came to be — rally by rally, court by court, Sunday by Sunday.

Founded in Pune · Est. 2021 · 800+ members and counting
Chapter 1

An idea travels and finds a home.

In the early 2000s, adult recreational tennis in Pune had limited outlets. Courts existed. Players existed. But there was little connective tissue between them. Tennis was often seen as either highly competitive or academy-driven, leaving casual adult players caught somewhere in between — wanting to play, unsure where to go.

Exposure to social tennis abroad helped introduce a different way of thinking about the sport. One of the early carriers of this perspective was Sudhir Pisal — an IT professional who encountered tennis not as a performance sport, but as a repeatable, learnable, social experience. The idea was simple but radical: tennis could be welcoming, forgiving, and shared.

"The idea itself mattered more than the individual. Tennis could be a space where you show up, belong, and come back."

When that mindset returned to Pune, it resonated immediately with people who had been looking for exactly the same thing. A different kind of court. A different kind of welcome.

Chapter 2

Choosing tennis. Finding people.

Like many working professionals, Sudhir and others had to make conscious choices about how they spent their mornings. Morning batches became gathering points, and familiar faces gradually turned into regular playing partners. Conversations after sessions often lasted longer than the matches themselves.

Around this time, WhatsApp quietly became an enabler. Initially used to simply coordinate play — "courts booked for Sunday, who's coming?" — it soon became a space for exchanging ideas, fixing games, and experimenting with formats. What felt like logistics slowly became the backbone of something much larger.

Early Days

The WhatsApp Era

Coordination moves to chat. A single group becomes the nervous system — fixture updates, new players, post-match banter.

The Question

What if Sunday could mean more than one match? What if people could stay all day and keep playing?

Chapter 3

The first Sunday that changed everything.

A defining moment came through conversations at venues like NCL, where Sudhir crossed paths with Capt. Santosh Chitnis — a passionate player preparing to return to life at sea. One suggestion from those discussions stood out above all others: longer Sunday sessions where players could play multiple matches without the elimination of a tournament format.

That question unlocked something. With help from friends like Arjun, and with no prior experience in organising events, a simple round-robin format was drafted late one night. No playbook. No template. Just intent and a notepad.

The next day, at Mhalunge Balewadi, a small group of players gathered to try something new. Skill levels varied. Experience varied. But the intent was shared. Matches flowed, players rotated, and the atmosphere felt unmistakably different from a typical tournament. There was competition — but no pressure to go home early.

Chapter 4

From one experiment to an unbroken rhythm.

The format worked because it was genuinely inclusive. Players stayed longer, played more, and left wanting to return. Nobody had to win to have had a great day. Word spread naturally — across Aundh, Baner, Sangvi, PCMC, Nigdi, and beyond Pune.

What followed wasn't rapid expansion or a viral moment. It was something more valuable: consistency. Week after week, Sundays became synonymous with social tennis. Organisation improved with each edition. Volunteers emerged. Responsibilities spread across more hands. Sudhir continued to play a coordinating role, but the community no longer depended on any single person.

"The community didn't need a leader. It needed people who kept showing up. And they did."

Chapter 5

Bridging community and professional tennis.

As participation deepened, so did exposure to the broader tennis world. Members of the community, including Sudhir, began volunteering at ATP Challengers and ATP 250 events held in Pune — gaining a close-up view of professional tennis operations and bringing those perspectives back into the community.

Sudhir also contributed as team manager for BIPL Rising Eagles, owned by Sameer Bhamre — creating closer interactions with coaches, juniors, and tennis parents across the city. These connections helped bridge the gap between recreational tennis and the broader ecosystem, benefiting members who wanted to go deeper into the sport.

The community grew not just in size, but in connection — to the sport's wider world, its professional players, and its coaches. What had started as a WhatsApp group for Sunday coordination was now a genuine part of Pune's tennis fabric.

What Tennisnuts Represents

Not defined by who started it.
Defined by who sustains it.

Sudhir Pisal's role — like that of many others — has been that of an enabler. He helped ideas move, formats take shape, and people stay connected. But the community itself belongs to everyone who stepped onto the court and chose to return.

And it continues, every time someone new joins in and feels that first clean shot cross the net.
